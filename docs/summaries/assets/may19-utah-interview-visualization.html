A Visualization

May 19, 2026 — Utah

A short story for visualization. Draws on the SLT-deck life-cycle stages, the Phase I priorities, the four Tier-1 STAR stories, the Attraction precision move, and the room rules from the May 19 Utah prep arc. Not a forecast — a rehearsal in narrative form.

Adam parked in his usual spot just before nine. The Utah office looked the same as any other morning — same security desk, same hallway, same elevator — but the air felt slightly different, the way it does when you know what's coming.

The conference room was the big one on the third floor. Patty was already there when he walked in, standing at the window with a coffee, and Michael was at the table opening his laptop. They both turned. Patty's smile was the smile she used when she was working — focused, warm, but doing two things at once.

"Adam. Thanks for making the time."

"Of course."

They shook hands. Michael clapped him on the shoulder lightly, the way he had at every Wednesday session for the last two months. The three of them sat — Patty and Michael on one side, Adam on the other, the laptop he'd brought set quietly to his left where he could reach it if needed.

"How was the drive in?" Patty asked.

"Short."

That got a laugh from Michael. Patty took a sip of her coffee and set it down.

"Okay. I want to walk the same shape we did on the 29th — life-cycle stages, then the Phase I priorities. Some of it we've covered, some of it'll be new ground. Anything you need before we start?"

"No, I'm good."

"Onboarding," she said. "Let's start there, since that's the live work."

Adam nodded. He'd known she'd open here, and he'd decided in the parking lot that he'd let her ask before he pulled anything up.

"Want me to pull it up?"

"I do."

He opened Reach360 and turned the laptop so both of them could see. Track 1 — Intro to PrePass loaded, the eight-module structure in the dashboard. He kept his opening narration tight: Phase I Onboarding deliverable, eight modules, ninety minutes, org-wide, designed modular from day one. He named the origin — his tolling Articulate course that Stephanie had shared with SLT, Michael reaching out directly in early March. He kept Michael's name in the work without crowding it.

When he clicked into the weekly tracker, Michael leaned forward. "That's the cadence we land on every Wednesday. He shares it the morning of; I see it before our sync." He looked at Patty. "It's the most disciplined working rhythm I've had on a project that wasn't formally scoped yet."

Adam pulled up M3 in Reach — Company History, live since April 23. He talked through the pyramid framing, how it laid alongside the People Team's live session rather than against it. He clicked over to the M5 storyboard, named the Business Acumen overlap — M5, M6, M7 doing double duty against two Phase I priorities at once. Patty wrote something in her notebook.

He closed with the throughput line: three live, four in build, the iteration accelerating as time-in-Articulate compounded. Then he stopped clicking.

Patty was quiet for a second. Then: "Did this ship the way you wanted it to?"

There it was. He'd practiced the answer aloud Saturday night and he didn't rush it.

"No. The original cadence had Track 1 fully stood up before May 4 kickoff. We hit three; we didn't hit eight. Running the new-hire class concurrently with the build was a real constraint. M5 and M6 have cross-functional content gaps the current scope can't unblock. The durable signal I'd point to is throughput acceleration — these first three took three months; M2, M5, M7 will land faster. The architecture is still right. The bottleneck was bandwidth and content, not design. Both resolve when the function has a dedicated seat."

Patty nodded once, slowly. "Yeah."

· · ·

She moved on. "Development. AI Literacy specifically. Talk me through what it looks like in practice."

He did. He opened with the April 30 Tamie demo — the Copilot search, the KB grounding, the FreshService-to-D365 conversion fact that had surfaced for both of them in the same moment. He named the daily AI workflow at PrePass, the four layers of it, without listing them like bullet points. He landed the three results in escalating order, and at the third one — the working definition of AI Literacy as function-by-function, role-specific, anchored in the org's actual tools — Patty's pen stopped moving.

"What are you actually using?" she asked.

He'd rehearsed this one too.

"Copilot's the org-approved layer. I work it inside Teams and the FG pipeline. Functionality there is more constrained than the public cloud tools, which is worth knowing. I've also worked across other LLM platforms and run open-source models on my own hardware to keep cross-tool fluency current — understand where each platform's capabilities differ. The fluency I want for the function is cross-tool literacy plus operational judgment about when each tool fits — and the AI Literacy curriculum is going to need to address governance, access, and tool selection as one of its real jobs, not an afterthought."

Patty looked at Michael. Michael nodded almost imperceptibly. She didn't push further on the tooling question, and Adam felt the small relief of a probe that didn't go where it could have.

· · ·

"Skills and Capability Assessments," she said next. "You ticked one-but-not-both on the screener. Talk me through that."

He took the breath he'd practiced.

"Skills and Capability Assessments is one of the four Phase I priorities signaled to this seat. On the application screener I ticked 'experience with one but not both' — I claimed the upskilling-program side; I disclaimed the formal, org-wide capability-assessment side. That tick was deliberate. The honest read is I haven't owned a formal org-wide skills matrix that every function gets scored against. The bridge to it is real, though."

He walked the three examples. Track 2 prioritization, where the build sequence was a prioritized gap map. Track 1 audience-content mapping with Michael, where the modular architecture was gap analysis at the audience level. Then he came to Universal Agent.

"The courseware I inherited as Call Center trainer was skeletal — a Word outline and a planner, the existing CS and Billing tracks pasted at the seam. Across subsequent classes I filled in timing, cut duplicates, built bridge content where later modules touched earlier learned material, and extended a five-week merger into a six-week integrated program. Nearly four of the six weeks are floor work now. Decisions sourced from my read as a former CS agent, input from agents and TLs and supervisors, six years as a formal trainer at eBay plus twelve more training-adjacent before that."

He paused.

"Honest read: closest thing in my recent work to a capability assessment for a single role family — without the formal validation layer that would make it one. The validation layer is what Michael and I would build."

Michael said, "He's right about that. The diagnostic was running. The framework wasn't."

Patty wrote something. "Are you familiar with ADDIE?"

"Yes — Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, Evaluate. The 'analyze' phase is the up-front diagnostic that has to happen before design and build. That's the phase my work has lived in, even when I didn't have the formal label for it. I learned this craft by doing it; the formal vocabulary came later. The work I've been describing is ADDIE analyze-phase work that just happens to be informal. The formal validation layer is what Michael and I would build out, with the Council operating it."

She set her pen down and looked across the table at him.

"I appreciate the precision."

He didn't say anything to that. He'd been told a long time ago not to fill a pause that wasn't his to fill.

· · ·

Patty came back to Attraction. "I know you disclaimed that one on the 29th. Anything you'd add now?"

"Disclaimer still stands. But worth flagging — I do have one artifact in the stage. PrePass Essentials Introduction. Standalone Rise module we give Utah call center candidates pre-hire to introduce them to PrePass. Not a primary lane I'd claim. The function's discipline reaches across stages where artifacts make sense."

He thought about the Utah-resonance line — the candidates seeing it are sitting in this building. He decided against it. The substance was enough.

Patty smiled at the corner of her mouth.

· · ·

She circled back to Development one more time. "Track 2. Walk me through."

He ran it. Four modules live in Reach, six more in active build, the AI-aided authoring loop, Storyline for the long-form modules and Rise for the procedural reference content, LMS deployment as a real step. He landed the measurement honesty cleanly — completion rates today via Reach reporting, the time-to-proficiency data that might already be available in the underlying systems but had never been compiled or analyzed for this purpose, Geno on the reporting side as the partner he'd want to bring in once the bandwidth opened up.

"The pace honest read," he said. "About five months on the calendar from first module live. Less than that in actual build time — both the February new-hire class and the May class pulled me off the pipeline as the primary gravity. The work happens in the windows between class deliveries. The seat formalizing is what removes the trade-off and lets the pace stay steady."

He paused at the close. He said it the way he'd practiced — like he was already in the seat.

"The Specialist seat needs both halves. Convener and builder. Track 1 with Michael shows the convener half. Track 2 shows the builder half: end-to-end ownership in a function that previously had no self-paced pipeline, an AI-aided authoring loop that ships modules quickly between class deliveries, without compromising KB accuracy."

Patty looked at her notes for a moment. Then she capped her pen.

· · ·

"Two questions before we wrap," Adam said. "First — if I'm in this seat at 90 days, what's the first observable deliverable that tells you it was the right hire?"

She thought about it. "AI Literacy curriculum landing in one function. Probably Customer Care, since you've got the diagnostic baseline. Documented method that the Council can take to a second function in the next 90 days. I want to be able to point at it and say, the seat works."

"Second — timing for moving to a formal offer after we wrap today?"

"End of the week. I want to talk to Chris this afternoon and have something in writing to you by Friday."

He nodded. He didn't push on it. Michael was looking at him across the table with the expression Michael wore when something he'd been working on was about to land.

They stood. Handshakes — Michael's hand on his shoulder again, Patty's grip firmer than her smile. He gathered the laptop and walked out.

The hallway felt different than it had ninety minutes earlier. Not different because he knew the outcome — he didn't yet, not officially — but different because he'd walked in carrying something and walked out without it. The prep was behind him now. What he'd been carrying for ten days had been delivered.

He took the stairs down. By the time he reached the parking lot he was already thinking about Friday.
